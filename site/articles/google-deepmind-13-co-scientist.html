中文摘要
Google DeepMind 发布基于 Gemini 的多智能体科研系统 Co-Scientist，通过"想法锦标赛"机制生成、辩论和评估假设，论文发表于 Nature。实测将 6 个月的文献分析压缩至数天，成功筛出癌症药物 vorinostat 可阻断肝纤维化损伤反应达 91%。

Co-Scientist is a multi-agent AI system built on Gemini by Google DeepMind, designed to act as a research collaborator that simulates the scientific method. It uses a multi-agent "idea tournament" architecture to generate, debate, and evaluate hypotheses, with all claims deeply verified and supported by clickable citations. The research paper describing the system was published in Nature in 2026.

Uncovering repurposed medicines to fight liver fibrosis

Liver fibrosis is a scarring process that can lead to cirrhosis, which causes more than 1.4 million deaths each year. Geneticist Gary Peltz at Stanford University School of Medicine used Co-Scientist to accelerate the hunt for medicines that can slow, stop, or reverse it.

In research published in Advanced Science, Peltz's team asked Co-Scientist to propose three drug-repurposing candidates and explain its reasoning. Peltz also identified two candidate drugs himself based on their notable presence in the liver fibrosis literature. All five were then tested in a fibrosis testbed of live human liver cells.

The results were striking. Peltz's two drug picks showed no benefit against fibrosis. By contrast, two of the three candidates selected by Co-Scientist blocked fibrosis and promoted regeneration of liver cells. One of these drugs had only been linked to liver fibrosis in a handful of papers — a needle in the haystack of scientific literature.

Co-Scientist's standout pick was the cancer drug vorinostat. In Peltz's experiments, it blocked 91% of a damage response that drives liver scarring. Co-Scientist's suggestions pointed toward drugs that reshape gene activity, rather than targeting a single fibrosis pathway — an approach Peltz argues deserves serious consideration as a new generation of anti-fibrotic medicines.

"Co-Scientist feels like a collaborator that's read everything available about biomedical science, with the reasoning capabilities to find the connections that we're currently missing."

— Professor Gary Peltz, Stanford University School of Medicine

Fast-tracking genetic leads to reverse cellular aging

Two of the biggest bottlenecks in aging research are deciding which genetic pathways to test and making sense of the vast data those experiments produce. Biologists Omar Abudayyeh and Jonathan Gootenberg at the Abudayyeh–Gootenberg Lab used Co-Scientist to help blast through both.

Their lab runs huge genetic screens that flip thousands of genes on or off, then reads how cells respond. The goal is to find changes that push cells away from senescence — a damaged state linked to aging — and toward a youthful state in skin, hair, and muscle tissues.

Co-Scientist helped on two fronts. First, it generated leads: when asked to trawl the scientific literature for factors that might reverse aging, it scanned tens of thousands of papers, considered a multitude of hypotheses, and ultimately proposed more than 20 novel, plausible genetic factors to test. Lab tests validated some of Co-Scientist's hypotheses, with recommended factors successfully driving cells into a younger state with improved overall function.

Second, Co-Scientist dramatically sped up the follow-through. Connecting large-scale screening results to years of scattered scientific literature can take a researcher up to six months. With Co-Scientist analysing screening data alongside the literature, that work is slashed to just a few days.

"Using Co-Scientist feels like having a team of 50 people at your disposal, doing all the work within a day, which isn't something we can otherwise do with our lab."

— Omar Abudayyeh, Principal Investigator

Accelerating discovery of liver disease mechanisms

At the University of Edinburgh, bioengineer Filippo Menolascina used Co-Scientist to comb the literature for overlooked mechanistic links in metabolic dysfunction-associated steatohepatitis (MASH), a complex liver disease where single-target drugs fall short.

In one emblematic case, Co-Scientist tackled a live, practical question: why does resmetirom — a recently approved treatment for a specific stage of MASH — only help a narrow slice of eligible patients? The system produced a hypothesis pinpointing the NLRP3 inflammasome as the specific molecular bridge coupling inflammation and metabolism — a connection never previously assembled into a single, actionable explanation. The hypothesis was later experimentally verified and could pave the way for targeted dual-therapies.

"Co-Scientist feels like a jetpack for scientists, powering up our ability to identify promising mechanisms. I think we're on the brink of a scientific revolution that will significantly shorten the iteration cycles needed to achieve breakthroughs."

— Professor Filippo Menolascina, University of Edinburgh

Opening new paths in aging research

At Calico Life Sciences, head of AI/ML Matt Onsum and principal scientist Katherine Labbé used Co-Scientist to connect scattered findings across the biology of aging. The biology literature is full of mixed-quality findings and results that do not replicate; Co-Scientist impressed Calico's experts with its scientific discernment, helping cut through that noise.

One example came from Calico's work on the integrated stress response (ISR), a protective cellular mechanism that can contribute to disease when persistently activated. The team used Co-Scientist to generate a novel hypothesis about how the ISR is regulated by metabolism — which changes with age. Researchers interacted with Co-Scientist to refine the experimental design and feed in new information as results emerged, leading to new findings with important implications for the role of ISR in health and disease.

How Co-Scientist works: the idea tournament

Co-Scientist simulates the scientific method through a multi-agent "idea tournament" architecture:

  • Collaboration: Works with researchers to define a specific research challenge or question.
  • Generation: Multiple agents scan millions of scientific papers to generate diverse candidate hypotheses.
  • Debate and evaluation: Agents debate and evaluate the hypotheses against each other in a tournament format.
  • Verification: Claims are deeply verified and supported by clickable, traceable citations.
  • Iteration: Researchers can feed in new experimental results to refine hypotheses in real time.

Part of Gemini for Science

Co-Scientist is the "Hypothesis Generation" engine within Google's broader Gemini for Science initiative, alongside AlphaEvolve (Computational Discovery) and NotebookLM (Literature Insights). The Co-Scientist research paper was published in Nature in 2026.

The system is being deployed in collaboration with over 100 institutions, including Stanford University, Imperial College London, The Crick Institute, Calico Life Sciences, Daiichi Sankyo, Bayer Crop Science, and the U.S. National Labs as part of the Department of Energy's Genesis Mission. A trusted tester community ranging from PhD students to Nobel laureates stress-tests the system against complex real-world challenges.
